Why Leave a Perfectly Good Career?

I was a good Chef.
I even used my GI Bill to go to a culinary school with a prestigious name; Le Cordon Bleu. . . . . .

Those first few quarters I attended really did give me a good introduction to a few key skills I would later need. And they did still do a good job of getting my foot in the door with job placement right from the start. Not long after I decided to leave the program, the school was later sued for fraud and closed down nationwide. Of course they were. Just my luck.
Regardless, the experience did still help get me off the ground as a Chef.

I had my favorite parts of the resulting culinary career; those peaceful mornings in the bakeshop.
That nostalgic feeling of teamwork and coordination in a fine dining style course firing.
And, as a Chef in charge of the kitchen, the affirmation and satisfaction of watching my food being enjoyed and praised by well-paying guests and locally famous food critics.

I also had my struggles, like many. Restaurant culture and lifestyle are well known for not being of the highest quality at all times when compared to more "upstanding" professions.

And then, covid happened. The world shut down along with the overwhelming majority of the hospitality industry.
This was my chance to find something even better. Something more sustainable for the long term.

After all, when the seven day week loses all meaning and you're on your feet 60-80 hours each "week", the clock is ticking.
No one can do that forever. Burnout isn't just a risk. It's inevitable.

Subtle Beginnings

My first coding related experience started when I was in the Army as a combat school instructor. A nice cooldown placement after dodging bombs and bullets for Uncle Sam.. . . . .


I used and helped maintain several spreadsheets related to the school; such as for student scores and performance data. Learning what functions were and seeing the power I could have if I learned them well.

Functions are cool.

It wasn't until years later that I would again encounter Excel functions in any meaningful way.
As a Chef I used a spreadsheet to make well designed schedules for my staff that also kept us within budget and somewhat agile when we expected an especially slow or busy week ahead. Once again, the efficiency and power of programming tickled those dopamine factories in my brain. This of course then led to me developing other spreadsheets that helped me track the quantity and value of my inventory as well as alert me when I might be getting overcharged by my purveyors for ingredients or equipment.
In fact, it was in part due to my spreadsheets that I was able to discover a pattern of overcharging in the invoices. From that data I successfully(!) negotiated for a $40,000 credit from one of the purveyors at one of the restaurants I worked with and a renegotiation of the contract with set-in-stone, transparent pricing margins disclosing the originating purchase price to compare to their resale price.
And $40k is no small sum for a small business like that.

Business tech.

In early 2020, when the pandemic set fire to the restaurant industry, I used the popup food business I was already working on to shift gears and launch a private events service.
Of course, I had a spreadsheet for everything. I had a profit and loss spreadsheet to track overall spending and revenue. I had another that gave me event pricing to keep me within my margins and nail down an appropriate price point for every plate, and every guest at each event. Everything was data driven and predictable. I hit my target margins exactly, every time. No penny went unnoticed. I also made my first website to establish a web presence as well. This was the first time I had ever done anything like web development. Of course, at the time I had no coding experience beyond simple Excel spreadsheets, so I used Squarespace to make it.

"So that's where ads come from."

I set up social media on Facebook and Instagram, generated and posted content. I learned how to navigate and use the Facebook Business Manager, run targeted ads for the people I wanted to reach and connect everything installing Facebook Pixel code on my website. My ads on each platform linked to my website with a custom URL for traffic analytics.
This, I think, is where the obsession really began. I found myself enjoying the tech so much more than the cooking. I would fantasize about doing just the web and social media full time. Ah, wouldn't that be nice to just do this all day every day? But no, probably not. I'm sure you need a whole master's degree for that and I have no interest in taking on that kind of debt, financial or otherwise. If only, right?

"Wouldn't it be nice?"

Well, fast forward about a year. I had tried my hand at insurance, working as an independent broker, knowing very well that I had no business doing sales work at that level of intensity.
I used the same marketing strategy as before, but I was tackling it from two ends. I used the same web + social + ads combo for lead generation and also purchased third party leads as well. And that had actually worked just fine and I did fairly well; better than most, not nearly as good as some.
But, I really just had no long term aptitude for that kind of work. I enjoyed the detail oriented aspect of what insurance is, learning all about it and all the details that govern insurance, how it all works and what types work best for certain situations and goals. I was a fantastic advisor and I closed far more deals than I lost. Sure, I liked being able to win clients and influence people to do better for themselves.
I handle sales quite well, just not when it involves 500+ approaches every day. And I also just don't enjoy that level of constant social interaction. Some people do, I just don't.
No, my passion was in the impersonal details. It was always the tech that I enjoyed more than everything else. There was just nothing better than that moment when you sit down, log in, and crack into a new project. Except for seeing the finished product, of course.

My Coding Journey


"It's too expensive. You can't do it."

A little over a year later, I had accepted my reality and was still determined to find my . . . . .

next career.
I had survived the military and a combined 27 months of combat overseas.
I survived the big city fine dining culinary scene, more or less, and the global and industrial crises that followed.
Now, I had to pick myself back up, and move on, and continue to push forward. I had to find my next (and please please be the last) career.

So, I do my research. I look into all the VA programs with my GI Bill having been chewed up and swallowed by a fraudulent culinary school.
I looked into trade schools, apprenticeships and union training programs. And that all seemed fine. I was especially interested in the local electricians union. But at the end of the day it really just didn't motivate me enough to take the leap. Then, right around November of 2021, my wife casually mentioned to me a Tiktok video she saw about coding and coding bootcamps.

"Or maybe, I can?"

Again, like I said before, I had written off web development as a career thinking the barrier of entry, the time requirement, the academic fluff and outrageous student debt I'd surely need to take on to get a computer science degree (and even to some degree, a bootcamp certification), was just too expensive for me.

But I decided to do some research. I'm finding youtube videos left, right and center. Blog articles outlining all the ways you can learn the skills you need to actually break into the tech industry as a developer.

I had seen all I needed to see. I'm in. Let's go.

New Purpose.

After weighing all the options I could find and reading all the bootcamp reviews and “If I could do it all over again” articles and videos, I had decided on the self taught route. With my personality, learning style and work ethic, I'm perfect for that.
Looking at all of the self study resources I could find, I finally settled on The Odin Project. I liked them because they start you off in a unix environment pushing git commits to github from day one. That was pretty cool. I went through the entire Foundations course, using a few Udemy resources as needed; anytime I felt stuck or just wasn't getting it.

React, or.. React?

But as I finished that section there was a choice to be made. The Odin Project is then forked into two options for full stack learning and frameworks: React and Ruby on Rails, or React and Node.js.
Ok, well, then I needed to figure that out. More research. I found out there are several highly popular frontend and backend frameworks I could choose from. React seemed to be the most popular. It's a javascript library that was created, and now maintained by Facebook.
And. It's. Everywhere.

Angular?

I noticed that Angular was another popular one. It's a very well supported framework based on a language called Typescript that was created, and now maintained by Google. What's Typescript? Come to find out, it's coding bliss, that's what it is. And the Angular way just feels so right to me.

Now, don't get me wrong. I fully expect to learn and get comfortable with other frameworks, tech stacks and programming languages later on. From what I've seen it's pretty much inevitable that I'll need to move forward at some point, probably sooner rather than later. But Angular has been very, very good to me. I'm still happy with it and I think I chose my first framework wisely.

The Angular Way.

Anyway, since Angular wasn't covered by tOP, I got started with Max Schwarzmuller of Academind. I took a brief dive into Typescript, enough to get as comfortable with it as I was with vanilla javascript, and then went right into his complete guide to Angular and his MEAN Stack course where I learned about:

I didn't even realize until I wrote all that out just how far I've come. And I've used the vast majority of that to create this portfolio piece you're reading this on. Pretty cool huh?
